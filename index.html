Module Overview

This module opens the unit by establishing the foundational Romantic move: the turn toward the natural world as a site of feeling, meaning, and power. Students encounter nature not as backdrop but as protagonist — a force that overwhelms, consoles, terrifies, and transforms. By comparing how Keats, Shelley, and Coleridge each construct the natural world, students develop their first vocabulary for the period's sensibility.

Texts in Focus
  • Keats: To Autumn, On the Sea, Ode to a Nightingale
  • Shelley: Ode to the West Wind, Mont Blanc, The Cloud
  • Coleridge: The Rime of the Ancient Mariner, Frost at Midnight, Kubla Khan
Assessment Objectives
  • AO1 — Informed personal response to texts
  • AO2 — Literary and historical context of Romanticism
  • AO3 — Analysis of form, structure, and language
  • AO4 — Clear, organised written communication
Key Questions
  • What does it mean for nature to be "sublime"?
  • How does each poet position the self in relation to the natural world?
  • What does the natural world reveal that ordinary experience cannot?
Sequence & Duration
  • Suggested duration: 3–4 lessons

Moves from sensation to analysis
II
Close Reading
The Sublime in Three Registers
Grounding the theme in specific textual detail
Activity A — Annotated Reading: Ode to the West Wind
~30 min Individual then Pair AO3

Shelley's Ode to the West Wind is often read as one of the period's most formally ambitious encounters with natural force — a poem where the architecture of the verse (terza rima, the five-canto structure, the concluding couplet of each section) feels inseparable from what the poem is trying to say. Before any comparative work, students need to sit with this poem long enough to feel how it is built, and to form their own sense of what the wind means in it.

O wild West Wind, thou breath of Autumn's being,
Thou, from whose unseen presence the leaves dead
Are driven, like ghosts from an enchanter fleeing,

Yellow, and black, and pale, and hectic red,
Pestilence-stricken multitudes: O thou,
Who chariotest to their dark wintry bed

— Percy Bysshe Shelley, Ode to the West Wind (Canto I, ll. 1–6)
01
Read Canto I in full. Mark every word that attributes agency or personhood to the wind. What kind of entity is the wind in this canto?
02
Trace the shift across the five cantos. How does the speaker's relationship to the wind change? Where does the poem's emotional centre of gravity lie?
03
Focus on the final two lines: "If Winter comes, can Spring be far behind?" — Is this consolation or question? How does the poem's form prepare you for this ending?
Q1
Shelley addresses the wind directly throughout the poem. What does this apostrophe — speaking to something non-human — allow the poem to do that a descriptive mode could not?
Q2
The wind is both "Destroyer and Preserver." Some critics read this as characteristic of the Romantic period's tendency to find meaning in contradiction rather than resolution. Do you think the poem holds these opposites in genuine tension, or does it eventually tip toward one over the other?
AO3 Focus
Students should be able to comment specifically on terza rima, the volta structure within each canto, the use of apostrophe, and the accumulation of simile in Canto I. Move them away from identifying devices toward explaining what each device does in this specific poem.
Activity B — Comparative Passage Work: To Autumn & Frost at Midnight
~35 min Pair or Small Group AO1 · AO2 · AO3

One way into these two poems is to notice how differently they feel from Shelley's West Wind — and from each other. But rather than arriving at those differences with labels already attached, students should read the passages first and form their own sense of what kind of natural world each poem constructs, and what kind of self it positions in relation to that world.

A Note on Interpretation

Critics have characterised these three poems in various ways — Keats's autumn as "saturated and still," Coleridge's as "intimate and domestic," Shelley's as "kinetic and overwhelming" — but these are starting points for discussion, not settled descriptions. Students may find compelling reasons to push back on any of them. The comparative questions below are designed to open up that conversation rather than confirm a predetermined reading.

Season of mists and mellow fruitfulness,
Close bosom-friend of the maturing sun;
Conspiring with him how to load and bless
With fruit the vines that round the thatch-eves run;

— John Keats, To Autumn (ll. 1–4)

Therefore all seasons shall be sweet to thee,
Whether the summer clothe the general earth
With greenness, or the redbreast sit and sing
Betwixt the tufts of snow on the bare branch

— Samuel Taylor Coleridge, Frost at Midnight (ll. 65–68)
Q1
Both poems present autumn or winter with warmth rather than desolation. What does each poet's diction suggest about why nature can be consoling? Are the reasons the same?
Q2
Keats's poem is addressed to Autumn itself; Coleridge's is addressed to his sleeping child. How does the choice of addressee shape each poem's emotional logic?
Q3
Where does Shelley's West Wind fit in relation to these two poems? Is the sublime always overwhelming, or can it be gentle?
AO2 Link
This is a good moment to introduce Edmund Burke's distinction between the Beautiful and the Sublime (1757) as a brief contextual frame. Students do not need to engage with it extensively, but naming the distinction helps them articulate why these three poems feel different even when they are all about nature.
Contextual Note — The Burkean Sublime

Edmund Burke's A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful (1757) distinguished between the Beautiful — that which is small, smooth, and pleasing — and the Sublime — that which is vast, obscure, and productive of astonishment and terror. For Burke, the Sublime was a more powerful aesthetic experience precisely because it engaged the survival instinct at a safe remove.

Burke's framework is a useful critical lens rather than a definitive key. Applied to these poems, one might read Shelley's West Wind and Coleridge's Ancient Mariner seascape as leaning toward the Burkean Sublime — the vast, the impersonal, the vertiginous. But the framework has limits: it sits less comfortably with Keats's To Autumn and Coleridge's Frost at Midnight, which find depth and fullness in the small and seasonal rather than the vast and terrifying. Whether that means these poems belong to a different aesthetic category, or that Burke's binary is too neat, is itself a productive question. Later critics — including Kant, whose account of the sublime is quite different from Burke's — have complicated the model considerably. Teachers who want to pursue this further might consult M. H. Abrams's The Mirror and the Lamp (1953) for a broader account of Romantic aesthetic thinking.

Moves from analysis to argument
III
Comparative Task
Essay Preparation — Building a Comparative Argument
From close reading toward sustained critical writing
Activity A — Argument Mapping
~20 min Individual AO1 · AO4

Before writing, students map the comparative argument they would make in response to a sample question. The mapping exercise forces them to decide on a thesis — a claim about how the two poets they are comparing differ — before they begin marshalling evidence.

Sample Question (Paper 2 Section B format):

"In Romantic poetry, nature is less a source of comfort than a confrontation with forces beyond human understanding."

Compare how two of your set texts engage with nature in light of this view.

01
Choose two texts. You do not need to agree with the question — you can qualify, complicate, or disagree with it. Decide your position first.
02
Identify three points of comparison. These should be genuinely comparative — not "Text A does X, Text B does Y" but "Both texts X, but in different ways because..."
03
For each point, anchor to a specific passage. The passage should be short enough to quote closely (2–4 lines) and rich enough to sustain a paragraph of analysis.
04
Draft a thesis sentence. This is a single sentence that states your overall comparative argument — what is most interesting about the relationship between your two texts on this theme?
AO4 Focus
The mapping exercise is preparation for AO4: organising and presenting an argument clearly. Students who skip this step tend to write essays that accumulate points rather than develop an argument. The thesis sentence is the key intervention — insist on it being genuinely comparative rather than a description of the topic.
Activity B — Modelled Paragraph & Peer Response
~40 min Individual then Pair AO1 · AO2 · AO3 · AO4

Students write one fully developed comparative paragraph using the argument map from Activity A. The paragraph should open with a comparative claim, sustain close analysis of at least one quotation from each text, and end by connecting back to the question and the broader context of the period.

Self-check
Does your paragraph open with a claim that is genuinely comparative — not a description of what each poem does separately?
Self-check
Have you quoted specifically enough that your analysis could not be applied to any other poem? Is the quotation doing work in the paragraph, or is it decoration?
Self-check
Have you said something about what this reveals about the Romantic period — not just about the individual poem?
Teacher Note

Peer response works best when students are given a specific lens rather than general feedback. Ask pairs to focus only on the opening claim: is it a genuine comparative argument, or is it a topic sentence that could apply to either text independently? That single question surfaces most of the structural problems in comparative writing at this level.
